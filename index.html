Section B ‚Äî Practical Activity

Onion Peel Observation

What do we see under the microscope?
Cell Wall
Nucleus
Observation ‚Äî Onion Peel (Allium cepa)
The epidermal cells of an onion peel are arranged in a single layer. When stained with safranin or iodine, the cell walls and nuclei become visible under a compound microscope.
  • Cells are rectangular in shape
  • Each cell has a distinct cell boundary (cell wall)
  • A dark-staining nucleus is visible in each cell
  • Cells are arranged in a single layer (monolayer)
  • Large central vacuole may be visible in mature cells
  • No chloroplasts ‚Äî onion is a non-photosynthetic tissue

Why stain? Most cell contents are colourless. Dyes like safranin or methylene blue bind selectively to nuclei and cell walls, making them visible.

Recall
1. Who first observed and named cells? What did they look at?
Robert Hooke (1665) first observed cells in a thin slice of cork using a primitive microscope. He named them "cells" because they resembled small rooms (cellulae).
Understanding
2. Why was the invention of the microscope important for biology?
The microscope allowed scientists to magnify objects too small to see with the naked eye, making it possible to observe cells, microorganisms, and subcellular structures for the first time.
Analysis
3. What did Hooke actually observe ‚Äî living cells or dead cell walls? How do you know?
Hooke observed dead cell walls of cork cells. Cork is bark tissue made of dead cells whose walls remain after the living contents have dried out. He saw the box-like structure but not living content.
Section C ‚Äî Variety of Cells

Unicellular vs Multicellular

Cells are the building units of all life
Amoeba
Unicellular ‚Äî single cell performs all life functions. Changes shape to move and engulf food.
Bacteria
Unicellular prokaryote ‚Äî no nuclear membrane, simplest living cells. Some have flagella.
Humans
Multicellular ‚Äî ~37 trillion cells, each specialised for a function. Work together as tissues and organs.
Plants
Multicellular ‚Äî rectangular cells with rigid cell walls. Specialised cells: leaf cells, root cells, stem cells.
Key concept
Whether an organism has one cell or trillions, the cell is the basic unit of structure and function.
  • Unicellular: One cell does everything ‚Äî Amoeba, Paramecium, Bacteria, Yeast
  • Multicellular: Cells divide labour ‚Äî Animals, Plants, Fungi
  • More complex organisms have greater specialisation of cells
  • All cells, regardless of type, share basic features

Section D ‚Äî Basic Cell Structure

What is a Cell Made Of?

Three universal components of all cells
Plasma Membrane
Thin, flexible outer boundary. Selectively controls what enters and leaves the cell. Made of lipids and proteins.
Cytoplasm
Jelly-like fluid filling the cell. Contains water, salts, proteins, and all organelles. Site of many chemical reactions.

The control centre of the cell. Directs all cellular activities. Contains chromosomes (DNA) that carry genetic information.
Universal
These three components are found in all living cells ‚Äî from bacteria to plants to human nerve cells.

Section D ‚Äî Checkpoint

Test Yourself

‚ú¶ Checkpoint 3
Recall
1. What is the key difference between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells?
Prokaryotic cells lack a membrane-bound nucleus ‚Äî their genetic material lies free in the cytoplasm (nucleoid). Eukaryotic cells have a true nucleus enclosed by a double nuclear membrane.
Understanding
2. Why are bacteria classified as prokaryotes?
Bacteria lack a membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. Their DNA is a single circular chromosome lying free in the cytoplasm. They also have 70S ribosomes (smaller) and a cell wall made of peptidoglycan.
Application
3. A cell is found to have a nucleus surrounded by a double membrane. Is it prokaryotic or eukaryotic?
Eukaryotic. The double-membrane nuclear envelope is the defining feature of eukaryotic cells. Prokaryotic cells have no nuclear membrane at all.
Section E ‚Äî Cell Components

The Plasma Membrane

Selectively permeable boundary of the cell
  • Selectively permeable ‚Äî controls what enters and exits the cell
  • Made of a phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins
  • Hydrophilic heads face outward; hydrophobic tails face inward
  • Proteins act as channels, receptors, and carriers
  • Described by the Fluid Mosaic Model (Singer & Nicolson, 1972)
  • Fluid: phospholipids can move laterally
  • Mosaic: proteins embedded like tiles
Important
The plasma membrane is present in all cells ‚Äî prokaryotic and eukaryotic. In plant cells, it lies inside the cell wall. In animal cells, it is the outermost boundary.
Section E ‚Äî Cell Components

The Cell Wall

Rigid outer layer ‚Äî plants, fungi, bacteria only
Composition by organism
  • Plant cells ‚Äî cellulose (rigid, strong)
  • Fungi ‚Äî chitin
  • Bacteria ‚Äî peptidoglycan (murein)
  • Animal cells ‚Äî no cell wall
  • Fully permeable ‚Äî does NOT control passage of substances
  • Provides mechanical support, rigidity, and shape
  • Prevents cell from bursting in hypotonic solution (turgor)
  • Allows plant cells to maintain turgor pressure
  • Plasmodesmata ‚Äî tiny channels through cell wall for cell-to-cell communication

Section E ‚Äî Cell Components

The Nucleus

Control centre of the cell
  • Nuclear envelope: double membrane with nuclear pores
  • Nuclear pores: allow exchange of materials (RNA, proteins) with cytoplasm
  • Nucleoplasm: fluid inside the nucleus
  • Nucleolus: site of ribosome RNA (rRNA) synthesis; disappears during cell division
  • Chromatin: DNA + histone proteins; condenses into chromosomes during cell division
  • Human cells: 46 chromosomes (23 pairs)
  • Discovered by Robert Brown (1831) in plant cells
Functions
Controls all metabolic activities ¬∑ Carries hereditary information (DNA) ¬∑ Site of DNA replication ¬∑ Directs protein synthesis via RNA
Section E ‚Äî Organelles

Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

The cell's internal transport highway
Rough ER (RER)
  • Has ribosomes studded on surface
  • Site of protein synthesis
  • Transports proteins to Golgi apparatus
Smooth ER (SER)
  • No ribosomes on surface
  • Synthesises lipids and steroids
  • Detoxification of drugs and poisons (liver cells)
  • Stores calcium ions (muscle cells)
Common Functions ‚Äî Both ER types
  • Provide large surface area for chemical reactions
  • Form a transport network within the cell
  • Connected to the nuclear envelope
  • Help in cell membrane formation
ER is like the cell's highway system ‚Äî it connects the nucleus to the cell membrane and transports materials throughout.

Mitochondria

Powerhouse of the cell ‚Äî ATP factory

  • Found in all eukaryotic cells; more in high-energy cells (muscle, liver)

Section E ‚Äî Organelles

Plastids

Plant-cell only organelles ‚Äî pigment and storage
Chloroplasts
Contain chlorophyll (green pigment). Site of photosynthesis. Have thylakoid membranes (stacked into grana) and stroma. Own DNA and ribosomes. Most important plastid.
Chromoplasts
Contain coloured pigments (carotenoids ‚Äî red, orange, yellow). Give colour to flowers, fruits, and roots (carrot, tomato, petals). Attract pollinators and seed dispersers. Cannot do photosynthesis.
Leucoplasts
Colourless plastids for storage. Amyloplasts store starch (potato, rice), elaioplasts store oils, proteinoplasts store proteins. Found in non-green parts (roots, seeds).
Important
Plastids are found ONLY in plant cells. Like mitochondria, they have their own DNA and ribosomes ‚Äî supporting the endosymbiotic theory. Chloroplasts can inter-convert with chromoplasts (e.g. tomato ripening: green ‚Üí red).
Section E ‚Äî Organelles

Vacuoles, Lysosomes & Golgi

Storage, digestion, and packaging
Vacuoles
Membrane-bound sacs. Plant cells: one large central vacuole (up to 90% of cell volume) storing water, sap, waste. Animal cells: many small vacuoles. Food vacuoles in Amoeba engulf food.
Lysosomes
"Suicide bags" of the cell. Contain powerful digestive enzymes. Break down old organelles (autophagy), bacteria, worn-out cell parts. If they burst ‚Äî cell digests itself. Formed by Golgi apparatus. Rare in plant cells.
Golgi Apparatus
Stack of flat membrane sacs (cisternae). "Post office" of the cell ‚Äî receives proteins from RER, modifies, labels, and packages them for export or lysosomes. Makes glycoproteins and lysosomes.
Key Relationship
Proteins are made on Ribosomes (on RER) ‚Üí sent to Golgi apparatus for modification ‚Üí packaged as Lysosomes or secreted outside the cell.
Section E ‚Äî Organelles

Ribosomes

Smallest organelle ‚Äî site of protein synthesis
  • Smallest organelle ‚Äî not membrane-bound
  • Made of rRNA + proteins in two subunits
  • Eukaryotes: 80S (60S large + 40S small subunit)
  • Prokaryotes: 70S (50S + 30S) ‚Äî also in mitochondria and chloroplasts
  • Location: free in cytoplasm (make proteins for cell use) or on Rough ER (make proteins for export)
  • Site of translation ‚Äî reading mRNA to build protein chains
  • Present in all living cells ‚Äî prokaryotic and eukaryotic
Key Fact
Ribosomes are the only organelle found in both prokaryotes and eukaryotes. Their smaller size in prokaryotes (70S vs 80S) is exploited by antibiotics ‚Äî drugs like streptomycin target 70S ribosomes, killing bacteria without harming our 80S ribosomes.

Test Yourself

‚ú¶ Checkpoint 4
Recall
1. Which organelle is called the "powerhouse of the cell"? Why?
Mitochondria. They are the site of cellular respiration, producing ATP (adenosine triphosphate) ‚Äî the energy currency used for all cellular activities. High-energy cells like muscle cells have more mitochondria.
Understanding
2. Why are lysosomes called "suicide bags"?
Lysosomes contain powerful digestive (hydrolytic) enzymes. If the lysosome membrane ruptures inside the cell, these enzymes digest the cell's own contents ‚Äî effectively killing the cell. Hence they are called suicide bags. This also happens during programmed cell death (apoptosis).
Analysis
3. A plant cell has chloroplasts but a liver cell does not. Explain why.
Chloroplasts are needed for photosynthesis ‚Äî making food using sunlight and CO‚ÇÇ. Liver cells are animal cells; they cannot photosynthesise. They obtain energy by breaking down glucose through cellular respiration in mitochondria, not by making it in chloroplasts.

Understanding
2. Why do plant cells have a rigid shape while animal cells are irregular?

Section G ‚Äî Cell Processes

Osmosis & Cell Transport

Movement of water through semi-permeable membranes
Hypotonic Solution
Turgid / Swells
Water enters by osmosis. Plant cell ‚Äî wall prevents bursting ‚Üí turgor pressure. Animal cell ‚Üí may burst (haemolysis).
Isotonic Solution
Normal
Water moves equally in and out. No net change in cell volume. Cell is in equilibrium with surroundings.
Hypertonic Solution
Plasmolysis / Shrinks
Water exits by osmosis. Plant cell ‚Äî cytoplasm pulls away from wall (plasmolysis). Animal cell ‚Äî crenation (shrivelling).
Definition
Osmosis is the movement of water molecules from a region of higher water concentration (dilute solution) to a region of lower water concentration (concentrated solution) through a selectively permeable membrane.
Section H ‚Äî Cell Division

Cell Division

How cells reproduce ‚Äî mitosis and meiosis
MITOSIS ‚Äî Somatic Division
  • Occurs in somatic (body) cells
  • Produces 2 genetically identical daughter cells
  • Chromosome number maintained (2n ‚Üí 2n)
  • Purpose: growth, repair, replacement
MEIOSIS ‚Äî Reproductive Division
  • Occurs in gonads (testes, ovaries)
  • Produces 4 genetically varied daughter cells
  • Chromosome number halved (2n ‚Üí n)
  • Purpose: sexual reproduction (gametes: sperm, egg)
Section I ‚Äî NCERT Practice

NCERT Important Questions

‚ú¶ NCERT Exercises
NCERT Q1
Make a comparison and write down ways in which plant cells are different from animal cells.
Plant cells have: cell wall (cellulose), chloroplasts, large central vacuole, plastids. Animal cells have: centrioles, lysosomes, many small vacuoles. Plant cells are regular/rigid; animal cells are irregular. Both have: nucleus, mitochondria, ER, Golgi, ribosomes, plasma membrane.
NCERT Q2
How does an Amoeba obtain its food?
Amoeba obtains food by the process of endocytosis (phagocytosis). It extends pseudopodia (false feet) around the food particle, engulfs it forming a food vacuole. Lysosomes fuse with the vacuole and digest the food. Nutrients are absorbed; waste is expelled.
NCERT Q3
What would happen to the life of a cell if there was no Golgi apparatus?
Without the Golgi apparatus: proteins synthesised on ER cannot be modified, packaged, or transported. Cell secretion would stop. Lysosomes would not be formed, so intracellular digestion would fail. Cell membranes could not be renewed. The cell would eventually die.
NCERT Q4
Which organelle is known as the powerhouse of the cell? Why?
Mitochondria. They carry out aerobic respiration and produce ATP (adenosine triphosphate) ‚Äî the energy currency of the cell. ATP is used for all energy-requiring activities such as muscle contraction, active transport, and biosynthesis. Hence mitochondria are the powerhouse.
NCERT Q5
Where are proteins synthesised inside the cell?
Proteins are synthesised on ribosomes. Ribosomes may be free in the cytoplasm (produce proteins for internal use) or attached to the Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum (produce proteins for secretion or membrane use). The process of protein synthesis is called translation.

"Explain why plant cells do not burst when placed in a hypotonic solution, but animal cells do."
